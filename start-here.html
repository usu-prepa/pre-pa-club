These are the basics every Pre-PA student should understand early. Keep it simple, start organized, and double-check requirements on each program’s website.

PA Application Basics

1) CASPA Timeline (Typical Cycle)

  • Fall–Winter (year before): Research programs, confirm prereqs, start tracking hours.
  • Spring: Request letters (6–8 weeks ahead), draft personal statement, update resume.
  • Late April: CASPA opens (many programs begin accepting submissions).
  • May–June: Submit early when possible (verification + rolling admissions matter).
  • Summer–Fall: Supplemental apps + interviews.
  • Fall–Winter: Decisions + deposits (timeline varies by program).

Tip: “Early” usually means within the first 4–6 weeks after CASPA opens.

2) PCE vs HCE (Know the difference)

Programs categorize experience differently. Always check each program’s definitions.

  • PCE (Patient Care Experience): Direct, hands-on patient care (examples: CNA/PCT, EMT, MA, phlebotomy, ER tech).
  • HCE (Health Care Experience): Health setting experience that may be less hands-on (examples: scribing, unit clerk/front desk, some volunteer roles).

Tip: Track your hours as you go (date, role, site, supervisor, total hours). Don’t try to rebuild it later.

3) Common Application Mistakes

  • Not confirming prereqs for each program (they vary more than people think).
  • Waiting too long to ask for letters of recommendation.
  • Submitting CASPA late and losing rolling-admissions advantage.
  • Not tracking hours consistently (PCE/HCE/shadowing).
  • Personal statement that’s too generic (only “I want to help people”).

Tip: Your application should show growth + evidence, not just interest.

4) Letters of Recommendation (Quick Guide)

  • Who to ask: A PA/physician you worked with, a direct supervisor, and/or a professor (varies by program).
  • Ask early: Give at least 6–8 weeks.
  • Make it easy: Send your resume, draft personal statement, and a short list of points you hope they can speak to.
  • Follow up: Polite reminder 1–2 weeks before the deadline.

Tip: Strong letters come from people who can describe your work habits and patient care—not just that you’re “nice.”
